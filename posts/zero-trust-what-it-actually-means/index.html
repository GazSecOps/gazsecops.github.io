Zero Trust: What It Actually Means When You Have to Implement It

Vendor: Our zero trust platform provides seamless, frictionless, AI-powered security across your entire estate.

Sysadmin: What does it actually do?

Vendor: It verifies every request, every time, everywhere.

Sysadmin: So does a firewall rule and a login page. What does yours do differently?

Vendor: …it has a dashboard.

Zero trust has become the most abused term in security marketing. Every vendor has a zero trust product. Every slide deck has a zero trust slide. Every CISO has a zero trust initiative.

Most of them are buying a product and calling it a strategy.

Zero trust is not a product. It's the slow, painful process of removing implicit trust from your network, one assumption at a time.

This post is about what zero trust actually looks like when you have to implement it. Not the conference version. The version where you’re staring at a network diagram, a list of legacy systems, and a budget that won’t cover half of what the vendors quoted you.

What Zero Trust Actually Means#

Strip away the marketing and you’re left with one idea: don’t trust anything by default.

Traditional networks have a perimeter. Inside the perimeter is trusted. Outside is untrusted. VPN puts you inside. Firewall keeps others out.

Zero trust says: there is no inside. Every request is untrusted until proven otherwise. Every time.

That’s it. Everything else is implementation detail.

The principles:

  1. Verify explicitly - authenticate and authorise every request based on all available data
  2. Least privilege - give the minimum access needed, for the minimum time
  3. Assume breach - design as if attackers are already inside

None of this is new. Defence in depth, least privilege, and assume breach have been best practice for decades. Zero trust just says “actually do it, everywhere, not just at the perimeter”.

Why It’s Hard (The Honest Version)#

If zero trust were easy, everyone would have done it years ago.

Legacy systems don’t support it. That mainframe from 1997 doesn’t do OAuth. That ERP system authenticates with a shared password. That SCADA controller trusts anything on its VLAN. You can’t just flip a switch.

Networks weren’t designed for it. Flat networks with implicit trust between subnets. Servers that talk to each other because they always have. Firewall rules that say “allow all from 10.0.0.0/8” because someone needed it once and nobody removed it.

People don’t want it. Zero trust means more authentication prompts. More access requests. More friction. Users hate friction. Management hates user complaints.

It costs money and time. Identity infrastructure, microsegmentation, policy engines, monitoring. None of it is free. Most of it takes months to deploy properly.

Nobody owns it. Is zero trust a network project? An identity project? A security project? An everything project? Usually it becomes nobody’s project, which means it doesn’t happen.

Management: Where are we on the zero trust initiative?

Sysadmin: We’ve deployed an identity provider and started segmenting the network.

Management: So we’re zero trust now?

Sysadmin: We’ve removed about 5% of the implicit trust in our environment.

Management: But the slide said Q2.

Sysadmin: The slide was wrong.

The Three Pillars (In Practice)#

1. Identity: Know Who’s Asking#

Every request needs an identity. Not “this came from the office network”. An actual identity.

For users:

  • Strong authentication (MFA everywhere, no exceptions)
  • Conditional access (device health, location, risk score)
  • Session management (short sessions, re-authentication for sensitive actions)

For services:

  • Workload identity (not shared credentials, not API keys in environment variables)
  • Mutual TLS where you can
  • Short-lived credentials (tokens that expire, not passwords that don’t)

For devices:

  • Device identity and health checks
  • Compliance posture (patched, encrypted, managed)
  • Don’t trust the device just because it’s on the network

The hard part isn’t deploying an identity provider. It’s getting everything to use it. That “everything” includes the app from 2012 that only supports basic auth, the printer that can’t do 802.1X, and the vendor appliance with a hardcoded admin password.

2. Network: Stop Trusting the Wire#

Traditional: if you’re on the network, you can talk to anything else on the network.

Zero trust: every network flow is denied by default and explicitly allowed based on identity and policy.

Microsegmentation:

  • Workloads can only talk to what they need
  • East-west traffic is filtered, not just north-south
  • Policy follows the workload, not the IP address

In practice, this means:

  • Your database server only accepts connections from your app servers, not from “the server VLAN”
  • Your monitoring system can scrape metrics but can’t SSH to anything
  • Your CI/CD runner can deploy to staging but not production

The reality:

  • You’ll start with coarse segments and refine over time
  • You’ll break things when you tighten rules (have a rollback plan)
  • Some systems will never be properly segmented (document them, compensate elsewhere)
  • “Microsegmentation” often becomes “slightly less macro segmentation” and that’s still progress

3. Access: Every Request Gets Evaluated#

Not “you logged in this morning, so you’re trusted all day”. Every request, every time.

Policy evaluation:

  • Who is requesting? (identity)
  • What are they requesting? (resource)
  • From where? (network, device, location)
  • Is this normal? (behaviour, risk score)
  • Should they have access? (policy)

This requires a policy engine. Something that evaluates these signals in real time. Could be your identity provider’s conditional access. Could be a service mesh policy layer. Could be a proxy that makes decisions.

What it looks like in practice:

  • User on a managed, compliant device from the office: full access
  • Same user on an unmanaged device from a coffee shop: read-only, no downloads
  • Same user at 3am from a country they’ve never been to: blocked, alert triggered
  • Service A calling Service B: allowed if the service identity and scope match policy
  • Service A calling Service C: denied, no policy exists

Where to Start (Without Losing Your Mind)#

You can’t do everything at once. Pick the highest-value changes first.

Phase 1: Identity foundation (months 1-3)#

Get identity right first. Everything else depends on it.

  • Deploy MFA for all users (start with admins, then everyone)
  • Implement conditional access policies (block legacy auth, require device compliance)
  • Inventory service accounts and shared credentials (you’ll be horrified)
  • Start replacing shared credentials with workload identity where you can

What you’ll break: Legacy apps that don’t support modern auth. Plan for it.

Phase 2: Network visibility (months 2-4)#

You can’t segment what you can’t see.

  • Map network flows (what talks to what)
  • Deploy flow logging if you don’t have it
  • Identify implicit trust (flat VLANs, overly permissive firewall rules, “allow all” rules)
  • Document what should talk to what vs what currently does

What you’ll discover: Everything talks to everything. Half the flows are unnecessary. Nobody knows why the other half exist.

Phase 3: Coarse segmentation (months 3-6)#

Start cutting.

  • Separate environments (dev, staging, prod)
  • Isolate sensitive systems (databases, key management, admin interfaces)
  • Restrict management plane access (SSH, RDP, admin consoles)
  • Default deny between segments, explicit allow for known flows

What you’ll break: Something. Have monitoring and rollback ready.

Phase 4: Refine and extend (months 6+)#

Tighten.

  • Move from network-based rules to identity-based rules where possible
  • Implement per-service policies
  • Add behaviour-based signals (anomaly detection, risk scoring)
  • Extend to cloud workloads if you haven’t already

What you’ll learn: This never ends. That’s fine. Each iteration removes more implicit trust.

Common Failures#

Failure 1: Buying a product and declaring victory#

Management buys a “zero trust platform”. Deploys it. Declares the organisation zero trust. Nothing else changes.

The platform sits in front of some apps. Legacy systems bypass it. Network is still flat. Service accounts still use shared passwords.

Fix: Zero trust is a strategy. Products support it. They don’t replace the work.

Failure 2: Boiling the ocean#

Trying to microsegment everything at once. Replacing all authentication simultaneously. Rewriting every firewall rule in one change window.

Result: massive outage, rollback, zero trust initiative quietly shelved.

Fix: Start small. Pick one segment, one set of services, one environment. Prove it works. Expand.

Failure 3: Ignoring the human cost#

Zero trust adds friction. More prompts. More denied access. More “why can’t I reach this anymore?” tickets.

If you don’t manage this, users will find workarounds. Workarounds are usually less secure than what you had before.

Fix: Communicate changes. Explain why. Make the secure path the easy path where you can. Accept that some friction is the price of security.

Failure 4: No monitoring#

You’ve segmented the network. You’ve tightened access. But you’re not watching what’s happening.

Someone’s access gets blocked and they can’t do their job. Nobody notices for three days. Or: an attacker hits a deny rule and nobody sees the alert.

Fix: Monitor policy decisions. Alert on denials that look wrong (legitimate users blocked). Alert on patterns that look bad (repeated denied access from one source).

Here’s what that looks like in practice:

  • Identity logs: sign-in failures, MFA failures, risky sign-ins
  • Network logs: denied flows between segments (and changes in deny volume)
  • App logs: permission denied events, unexpected 401/403 spikes

If you’re using Prometheus, you can at least catch the obvious “everything is getting 403s” failure:

sum(rate(http_requests_total{status=~"401|403"}[5m]))
/
sum(rate(http_requests_total[5m]))

Alert if it jumps after a policy change.

For identity, you want alerts on:

  • repeated MFA failures for a user
  • repeated denied access from one IP or device
  • impossible travel / suspicious locations

If your IdP already does this (Entra ID, Okta), wire the alerts into your on-call and test them. Most places configure them once, never fire a test alert, and then discover at 3am that nobody receives the notifications.

Failure 5: Forgetting service-to-service#

Everyone focuses on user access. Nobody looks at service-to-service communication.

Your microservices talk to each other over plain HTTP with no authentication. Your batch jobs run as root with full network access. Your monitoring system has read access to everything.

Fix: Service identity matters as much as user identity. Mutual TLS, workload identity, scoped permissions. It’s harder than user auth but just as important.

One quick win: treat your CI/CD runners as hostile until proven otherwise.

  • Separate deploy identities for dev/staging/prod
  • No long-lived cloud keys on runners (use OIDC/workload identity)
  • Restrict what the runner can reach on the network

Most “zero trust” programmes ignore this and then get owned through a pipeline credential.

Cloud Makes This Easier (And Harder)#

Easier because:

  • Identity is built in (IAM roles, workload identity, managed identities)
  • Network segmentation is software-defined (security groups, network policies)
  • Policy engines exist (conditional access, IAM policies, OPA)
  • Short-lived credentials are the default for many services

Harder because:

  • More things to secure (VPCs, accounts, subscriptions, projects, clusters, functions, buckets…)
  • Shared responsibility means you own configuration
  • Multi-cloud means multiple identity systems
  • Developers can create resources without security review

The biggest cloud zero trust mistake: assuming the cloud provider handles it. They provide the tools. You have to use them correctly. A misconfigured security group is just a flat network with extra steps.

What Good Looks Like#

You won’t wake up one morning and be “zero trust”. But you can tell you’re making progress when:

  • New services deploy with identity and least-privilege access by default
  • Network access requires justification, not just “it’s on the VLAN”
  • You can answer “who accessed what, when, and why” for critical systems
  • Removing a user’s access actually removes their access (no lingering sessions, no shared passwords)
  • A compromised workstation doesn’t automatically mean a compromised network
  • Your blast radius analysis shows smaller blast radiuses than last year

The Boring Truth#

Zero trust is not exciting. It’s not a product launch. It’s not a transformation you complete in a quarter.

It’s removing one implicit trust assumption at a time. Enforcing MFA. Segmenting a subnet. Replacing a shared password with a service identity. Tightening a firewall rule. Adding a policy check.

Each one is small. Each one reduces risk. None of them make for a good conference talk.

The vendors will sell you a platform. The consultants will sell you a roadmap. The analysts will sell you a maturity model.

What actually works: pick the biggest implicit trust in your environment. Remove it. Pick the next one. Repeat.

That’s zero trust. Not a product. Not a project. A direction you move in, one boring change at a time.
